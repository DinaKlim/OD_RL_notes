## PHASE IDENTIFICATION

**Bornite**: pink (brown in oil), mostly isotropic, medium reflectivity.

**Pyrite**: light yellow, high reflectivity, isotropic.

VHN: 700+ (literature: >900).

The very small aggregates probably result in artificially low hardness values.
Hypidiomorphic.

**Chalcocite (Kupferglanz)**: bluish (more isotropic), VHN: 70; bluish, medium reflectivity, blue polishing scratches, isotropic.

**Hematite**: bright white, high reflectivity, anisotropic (occasional internal reflections in oil), elongated crystals.

**Goethite** (Nadeleisenerz): gray-brown, low reflectivity, anisotropic, reddish-brown internal reflections, needle-like, tufted, occurring adjacent to hematite.
± Siderite: gray, low reflectivity, very good cleavage, colorful anisotropy, strong internal reflections, X-ray confirmed.

## Textural description

The two main constituents are bornite and the sideritic gangue. Both occur in large, enclosed areas; however, small grains of bornite are also found within siderite, and siderite mostly occurs as veinlets within bornite.

Hypidiomorphic pyrite aggregates occur exclusively in bornite. Pyrite is enriched along the margins, but there it is partly replaced, together with bornite, by chalcocite (see literature reference 1, p. 494).

The siderite areas are surrounded by a hematite rim of varying thickness, which usually crystallizes in elongated, often pointed aggregates. In places, hematite has been secondarily altered into Nadeleisenerz.

Small amounts of chalcopyrite occur exclusively in the gangue, as rounded aggregates.

This ore is a Ni–As–Sb mineral paragenesis.
The methods used for phase identification were:

Reflected-light microscopy
Electron microprobe investigations (MS)
X-ray diffractometry

**Phase identification**

Phase 1:

This yellowish-pink mineral is an Sb-bearing nickeline (NiAs). The antimony content amounts to approximately 5–10% (result of the MS investigations). The nickeline is strongly anisotropic, shows high reflection pleochroism and high reflectivity, and is therefore unmistakable.

Phase 2:

This pink-violet mineral is an As-bearing breithauptite with high reflectivity, strong anisotropy, and very high reflection pleochroism. The arsenic content is about 5–10%.

Phase 3:

Using reflected-light microscopy, the following characteristics could be determined:

Pure white, isotropic mineral with high reflectivity. MS investigations yielded a Ni–As phase with low Sb content. The Ni:As ratio indicates a composition between NiAs₂ and NiAs₃. It is probably chloanthite 
(since NiAs₂ is anisotropic, and krutovite (NiAs₂) is very rare (isotropic, space group P2₁3).)

Phase 4:

White (with a yellowish tint), isotropic mineral with high reflectivity and galena-like cleavage {(100)}. Investigations with the microprobe (MS) yielded a Ni–As sulfide with low antimony content, but a relatively high As:S ratio.

The X-ray phase analysis clearly indicated gersdorffite. The As:S ratio can vary from NiAs₁.₆S₀.₄ to NiAs₀.₆S₁.₄. 

containing gersdorffite inclusions (NiAsS; Pa3) (ASTM No. 24520, Kostov 1982).

The As:S ratio can vary from NiAs₁.₆S₀.₄ to NiAs₀.₆S₁.₄. Ramdohr (1975) attributes the variable As:S ratio to submicroscopic intergrowths of gersdorffite with chloanthite. In this case, it is gersdorffite in which the As:S ratio is >1.

Phase 5:

This is an ullmannite with low arsenic content (approx. 5–10%) — NiSb(As)S.

In the sample, ullmannite shows a light grayish color, high reflectivity, and isotropy. The atypical light-gray color of the ullmannite may possibly result from “influence” by intergrown minerals.

Phase 6:

This phase differs microscopically from Phase 5 only in color. The color of Phase 6 shows a slight bluish tint. Microprobe (MS) investigations yielded the following contents:

Ni 30%, As 40%, Sb 20%, S 10%.

It is uncertain whether this phase is an arsenosulfide, or whether As is substituted by S. This phase could not be identified more precisely.
(Possibly it is a granular solid solution between ullmannite and gersdorffite with low Ni content.)

## Textural description:

Gersdorffite and nickeline form the principal constituents of this paragenesis.

Breithauptite occurs as inclusions within nickeline or is situated along its grain boundaries. More rarely, breithauptite forms fine inclusions in chloanthite.

Chloanthite, ullmannite, and Phase 6 commonly occur along the margins of the nickeline or as inclusions within it.

In the minerals nickeline, ullmannite, and chloanthite, a mineral zoning can be observed, possibly resulting from subsolidus mineralization with changing Ni/As, As/S, and As/Sb ratios.
